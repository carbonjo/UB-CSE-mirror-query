MS Program Tracks

The NYCE project team celebrates its CSE Demo Day Spring 2023 first-place award with CSE 611 advisor Alan Hunt. Photo credit: Ken Smith.

As the field of computer science evolves, our department continues to lead and grow with innovative academic tracks.

These tracks, which set UB apart from other computer science and engineering programs, allow students to gain a deeper understanding of specific facets of the field to stay ahead of ever-changing industry trends and customize their degree to their interests and career goals.

Students enrolled prior to Fall 2026

Students enrolled in the MS Computer Science and Engineering program prior to Fall 2026 may take advantage of the updated curriculum by changing their track to any of our new optional specializations. Interested students should contact csegradcoord@buffalo.edu. Students may not extend their study to pursue a specialization.

Learn more about the MS program specializations. 

Research Track

The Research track is more selective and provides more in-depth learning than the course-based tracks. Students take a series of breadth and focus courses in a computer science research area such as AI, hardware and networked systems, software and information systems, or theory and algorithms. Students then have the option of completing the program with a project (software, a proof, an AI model, hardware, etc.) to be presented at CSE Demo Day, or by defending a thesis. This track is ideal for current UB undergraduate students.

Please select the research track when applying.

  • Breadth: Theory is required. One 'Breadth' class chosen from three of the four remaining focus areas. 
  • Primary Focus: Two ‘Breadth’ or ‘Focus’ classes from a single area, including at least one 600-level class.
  • Secondary Focus: One ‘Breadth’ or ‘Focus’ requirement from a second area.
  • Seminar: One Seminar
  • Supervised Research: Five credits of supervised research or thesis guidance
  • CSE 501: Introduction to Graduate Study in Computer Science
Browse Breadth and Focus Courses

  • Artifact: A “production-quality” artifact (e.g., software, a proof, an AI model, or hardware).
  • Demo Day: A presentation of the project at CSE Demo Day.

  • MS Thesis: A conference-quality publication that describes a concept that advances the state of the art.
  • Thesis Defense: The student must form a committee including a primary advisor and at least one other CSE faculty member.

Artificial Intelligence/Machine Learning (AI/ML) Track

The AI/ML track is a course-based track where students take four core computer science courses, two electives, and three courses specific to artificial intelligence and machine learning. Students will also complete a capstone class (CSE 573) that brings together concepts from across the degree into a single, semester-long group project.

Only available to students enrolled prior to Fall 2026

  • CSE 521: Operating Systems
  • CSE 531: Algorithms
  • CSE 574: Introduction to Machine Learning
  • CSE 565: Computer Security

  • CSE 676: Deep Learning
  • CSE 560: Data Models and Query Languages
  • CSE 573: Computer Vision (capstone)

Students can take elective courses from any of the offered CSE courses with the following exceptions:
  • CSE 505: Fundamentals of Programming Languages
  • CSE 586: Distributed Systems
  • CSE 611: MS Project Development.  You must first apply to enroll in this course.  See more information, below.  Send resume to Alan Hunt
  • CSE 666: Biometrics Image Analysis (AI/ML Track, 3rd semester only)
  • CSE [701-749]: Seminars

Students can also take an approved out-of-major elective course at the 500 or 600 level including but not limited to:

  • MGO 660: Intro to Entrepreneurship
  • MGS 503: Introduction to Business
  • MGS 602: Global IT Management
  • EE 520: Quantum Computing
  • CSE/LIN/PHI/PSY 575: Intro to Cognitive Science
  • STA 545/CDA 541/EAS 506: Statistical Data Mining I

See the CSE Graduate Handbook for more information about out-of-major electives. Students may also earn up to three credits by doing an internship. Students are encouraged to take at least one elective in an interdisciplinary program or an internship.

Software and Hardware Systems (Systems) Track

The Systems track is a course-based track where students take four core computer science courses, two electives and three courses specific to software and hardware systems. Students will also complete a capstone class (CSE 562) that brings together concepts from across the degree into a single, semester-long group project.

Only available to students enrolled prior to Fall 2026

  • CSE 531: Algorithms
  • CSE 574: Introduction to Machine Learning
  • CSE 565: Computer Security

  • CSE 590: Computer Architecture
  • CSE 589: Modern Network Concepts
  • CSE 562: Database Systems (capstone)

Students can take elective courses from any of the offered CSE courses with the following exceptions:
  • CSE 505: Fundamentals of Programming Languages
  • CSE 586: Distributed Systems
  • CSE 611: MS Project Development.  You must first apply to enroll in this course.  See more information, below.  Send resume to Alan Hunt
  • CSE 666: Biometrics Image Analysis (AI/ML Track, 3rd semester only)
  • CSE [701-749]: Seminars

Students can also take an approved out-of-major elective course at the 500 or 600 level including but not limited to:

  • MGO 660: Intro to Entrepreneurship
  • MGS 503: Introduction to Business
  • MGS 602: Global IT Management
  • EE 520: Quantum Computing
  • CSE/LIN/PHI/PSY 575: Intro to Cognitive Science
  • STA 545/CDA 541/EAS 506: Statistical Data Mining I

See the CSE Graduate Handbook for more information about out-of-major electives. Students may also earn up to three credits by doing an internship. Students are encouraged to take at least one elective in an interdisciplinary program or an internship.

Why Choose CSE?

  • Research Centers, Labs, and Groups
    10/23/23
    Our research centers, labs, and groups provide abundant opportunities for exploring state-of-the-art research and working with sophisticated computing facilities.
  • Supercomputing
    6/27/19
    UB's Center for Computational Research (CCR) is considered one of the nation's leading supercomputing centers and supports high performance computing for departmental research in the areas of bioinformatics, medical image processing, virtual reality, and geographic information systems. 
  • A Vibrant Community
    7/15/19
    Our graduates are hired by the best companies and research laboratories.
  • Cutting-Edge Research
    2/10/20
    Our research areas span artificial intelligence, systems, and theory; they are well-funded by federal, state, and industrial sources. Our faculty work in multi-disciplinary and newly emerging fields, such as bioinformatics, computer vision, machine learning, and robotics.
  • Top-Notch Faculty
    1/28/25
    Be taught and mentored by faculty members who are outstanding teachers and internationally-recognized researchers.
  • High Salaries
    6/27/19
    A degree in Computer Science or Computer Engineering opens doors to exciting and rewarding careers that command high salaries. According to the US Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook, the median income for all computing and information technology occupations was $82,860 in May 2016.
  • Research Sponsors
    6/28/19
    Numerous world-class agencies fund our departmental research initiatives.
  • Document Analysis
    10/15/21
    Our affiliated Center of Excellence for Document Analysis and Recognition (CEDAR) is one of the world’s leading centers for research on interpreting scanned images. Among other accomplishments, CEDAR developed the systems that postal agencies around the world use for automatically sorting hand-addressed mail.
  • Established and Growing
    6/27/19
    Over the past three years, we have hired six new faculty members: in algorithms, databases, data mining, electronic commerce, natural language processing, and networks. Computer science was first organized as a department at UB in 1967—one of the first in the U.S.—and merged with computer engineering in 1998 to form the present department. 
  • Bioinformatics and Life Sciences
    5/20/19
    At UB, you can get into one of the hottest new research fields right at the beginning. Our faculty has direct involvement in the new $200 million New York State Center of Excellence in Bioinformatics and Life Sciences, particularly in the high-performance computing and computational science areas of this research.
  • Research Funding
    6/28/19
    Our faculty average $4.5 million annually in external research awards. We were ranked 26th among 165 U.S. PhD-granting Computer Science Departments for annual research support, according to a recent survey conducted by the National Science Foundation.
  • Industry Connections
    6/27/19
    Our industry partners include: Dell, IBM Corporation, IBM Digital, Kodak, Myricom, Praxair, Q-Chem, Sarnoff, Veridian Engineering Division, and Xerox.
  • Multidisciplinary
    6/28/19
    Our faculty work with researchers in chemistry, the life sciences, the pharmaceutical sciences, media study, geographic information science, and other disciplines where an interface with computer science is increasingly central.  Learn more about our collaborative research areas.
  • Experiential Learning Opportunities
    9/6/23
    Gain hands-on experience via graduate internships, research assistantships, and professional and student organizations.
  • Successful Alumni
    11/2/22
    Join the department’s network of accomplished alumni who have gone on to top graduate programs and employment with companies such as Bloomberg, Google, and Microsoft.
  • Community
    7/10/19
    Join a welcoming community.  You'll be part of a supportive, diverse, and dynamic environment.
  • Facilities
    10/18/21
    Use over 18,000 square feet of cutting-edge research labs, teaching labs, classrooms, and collaboration-focused meeting spaces.
  • Clubs
